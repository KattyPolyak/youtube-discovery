Can Fight Depression and Make You Happier</a></div></h2><p>Depression is a serious mental disorder that causes a significantly reduced quality of life.</p><p>It’s very common, as about 4.1% of people in the US currently meet the criteria for clinical depression.</p><p>In a Harvard study published in 2011, women who drank 4 or more cups of coffee per day had a 20% lower risk of becoming depressed (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21949167" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">38<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>).</p><p>Another study in 208,424 individuals found that those who drank 4 or more cups per day were 53% less likely to die by suicide (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8604958" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">39<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>).</p><blockquote class="css-pc7ote"><strong>Summary </strong>Coffee appears to lower your risk of developing depression and may dramatically reduce suicide risk.</blockquote></div><div id="threequarter-of-article"></div><div class="css-0"><span style="font-size:0"></span><h2><div><a name="section10"> 10. May Lower Risk of Certain Types of Cancer</a></div></h2><p>Cancer is one of the world's leading causes of death. It is characterized by uncontrolled cell growth in your body.</p><p>Coffee appears to be protective against two types of cancer: liver and colorectal cancer.</p><p>Liver cancer is the third leading cause of cancer death in the world, while colorectal cancer ranks fourth (<hl-trusted-source source="World Health Organization" rationale="Highly respected international organization" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="http://www.who.int/mediacentre/factsheets/fs297/en/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">40<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>).</p><p>Studies show that coffee drinkers have up to a 40% lower risk of liver cancer (<a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0016508507005689" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">41</a>, <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ijc.20989/full" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">42</a>).</p><p>Similarly, one study in 489,706 people found that those who drank 4–5 cups of coffee per day had a 15% lower risk of colorectal cancer (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22695871" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">43<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>).</p><blockquote class="css-pc7ote"><strong>Summary </strong>Liver and colorectal cancer are the third and fourth leading causes of cancer death worldwide. Coffee drinkers have a lower risk of both.</blockquote></div><div class="css-0"><span style="font-size:0"></span><h2><div><a name="section11"> 11. Doesn’t Cause Heart Disease and May Lower Stroke Risk</a></div></h2><p>It’s often claimed that caffeine can increase your blood pressure.</p><p>This is true, but with a rise of only 3–4 mm/Hg, the effect is small and usually dissipates if you drink coffee regularly (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22170367" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">44<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>, <hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21880846" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">45<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>).</p><p>However, it may persist in some people, so keep that in mind if you have elevated blood pressure (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14967827" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">46<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>, <a href="http://europepmc.org/abstract/MED/10556993" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">47</a>).</p><p>That being said, studies don’t support the idea that coffee raises your risk of heart disease (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16636169" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">48<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>, <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0939475306001694" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">49</a>).</p><p>On the contrary, there is some evidence that women who drink coffee have a reduced risk (<a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167527308008498" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">50</a>).</p><p>Some studies also show that coffee drinkers have a 20% lower risk of stroke (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23493733" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">51<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>, <hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21920945" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">52<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>).</p><blockquote class="css-pc7ote"><strong>Summary </strong>Coffee may cause mild increases in blood pressure, which usually diminish over time. Coffee drinkers do not have an increased risk of heart disease and have a slightly lower risk of stroke.</blockquote></div><div class="css-0"><span style="font-size:0"></span><h2><div><a name="section12"> 12. May Help You Live Longer</a></div></h2><p>Given that coffee drinkers are less likely to get many diseases, it makes sense that <a href="/nutrition/how-coffee-makes-you-live-longer" class="content-link css-5r4717">coffee could help you live longer</a>.</p><p>Several observational studies indicate that coffee drinkers have a lower risk of death.</p><p>In two very large studies, drinking coffee was associated with a 20% reduced risk of death in men and a 26% decreased risk of death in women, over 18–24 years (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18559841" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">53<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>).</p><p>This effect appears particularly strong in people with type 2 diabetes. In one 20-year study, individuals with diabetes who drank coffee had a 30% lower risk of death (<a href="https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007%2Fs00125-006-0435-9" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">54</a>).</p><blockquote class="css-pc7ote"><strong>Summary </strong>Several studies show that coffee drinkers live longer and have a lower risk of premature death.</blockquote></div><div class="css-0"><span style="font-size:0"></span><h2><div><a name="section13"> 13. The Biggest Source of Antioxidants in the Western Diet</a></div></h2><p>For people who eat a standard Western diet, coffee may be one of the healthiest aspects of their diet.</p><p>That's because coffee is quite high in antioxidants. Studies show that many people get <a href="/nutrition/coffee-worlds-biggest-source-of-antioxidants" class="content-link css-5r4717">more antioxidants from coffee</a> than from fruits and vegetables combined (<hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14988447" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">55<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>, <hl-trusted-source source="PubMed Central" rationale="Highly respected database from the National Institutes of Health" class="css-12hs4c5"><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14506489" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">56<span class="css-1mdvjzu icon-hl-trusted-source-after"><span class="sro">Trusted Source</span></span></a><span></span></hl-trusted-source>, <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1043661807000291" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="content-link css-5r4717">57</a>).</p><p>In fact, coffee may be one of the healthiest beverages on the planet.</p><blockquote class="css-pc7ote"><strong>Summary </strong>Coffee is rich in powerful antioxidants, and many people get more antioxidants from coffee than from fruits and veggies combined.</blockquote></div><div class="css-0"><span style="font-size:0"></span><h2><div><a name="section14"> The Bottom Line</a></div></h2><p>Coffee is a highly popular beverage around the globe that boasts a number of impressive health benefits.</p><p>Not only can your daily cup of joe help you feel more energized, burn fat and improve physical performance, it may also lower your risk of several conditions, such as type 2 diabetes, cancer and Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s disease.</p><p>In fact, coffee may even boost longevity.</p><p>If you enjoy its taste and tolerate its <a href="/nutrition/how-much-caffeine-in-coffee" class="content-link css-5r4717">caffeine content</a>, don’t hesitate to pour yourself a cup or more throughout the day.</p></div><div><section class="css-vaj3vd"><div class="css-1x6oboy"></div><div
